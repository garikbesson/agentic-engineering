# RFCs

RFC stands for **Request for Comments**.

In this repository, RFCs are lightweight proposals intended to start discussion before becoming reusable engineering knowledge.

This directory contains engineering ideas that are still evolving.

RFCs are proposals for new concepts, workflows, policies or repository improvements that have not yet become part of the knowledge base.

They exist to encourage discussion, experimentation and refinement.

---

# Purpose

RFCs help capture ideas before they become established engineering knowledge.

An RFC is not a finished document.

It is a place to explore:

- new engineering practices;
- workflow ideas;
- repository improvements;
- open questions;
- alternative approaches.

---

# What belongs here

Examples include:

- proposing a new engineering workflow;
- introducing a new policy;
- restructuring the repository;
- discussing a new engineering concept;
- exploring an idea that needs community feedback.

---

# What does NOT belong here

This directory is **not** for:

- finished engineering knowledge → `knowledge/`
- practical workflows → `playbooks/`
- engineering decision rules → `policies/`
- reusable engineering artifacts → `templates/`
- repository documentation → `docs/`

---

# Lifecycle

Most RFCs follow a simple lifecycle.

```text
Idea
    ↓
Discussion
    ↓
Refinement
    ↓
Accepted / Rejected
```

Accepted RFCs often become one or more documents elsewhere in the repository.

For example:

```text
RFC
    ↓
Knowledge

RFC
    ↓
Playbook

RFC
    ↓
Policy
```

Not every RFC needs to become part of the repository.

Some ideas will be rejected.

Others may inspire better solutions without ever becoming official guidance.

---

# Writing Guidelines

A good RFC should explain:

- the problem;
- the proposed idea;
- why it might be useful;
- open questions;
- possible next steps.

The goal is to start a conversation, not to present a final answer.

---

# Guiding Principle

RFCs exist to explore ideas.

Knowledge exists to explain ideas.

Playbooks exist to apply ideas.

Policies exist to formalize decisions.
